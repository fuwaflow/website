## The Neo is a great device, forgetting about the price

The MacBook Neo is lovely to use. The display is great, with 100% sRGB (accurate colours), it has a high pixel density, and gets reasonably bright. It comes in fun colours (silver, blush, citrus, indigo) and has a high quality aluminium unibody build. It's completely fanless (like the MacBook Air), and it works great for almost anything that you'd want to do on a computer.

## What else is out there?

Let's think about the price again. The MacBook Neo starts at £599 for the 256GB model (or £499 with the education discount), or £699 for the 512GB model (or £599 with the education discount). That puts it up against the base model consumer laptops from manufacturers like Dell and HP, like the HP OmniBook 3/5, the Dell 14 Laptop, and the Lenovo IdeaPad.
Those laptops have significantly worse displays, worse construction, are heavier, have worse trackpads, worse battery life, have noisy fans, the list goes on.

## How did Apple reach the price?

They re-used their A18 Pro chips that were used in the iPhone 16 Pro. Since they already had the platform for running macOS on mobile chips, (the first M1 chip was based on the A12Z used in the iPad Pro) it wasn't too hard to repurpose those chips. That helped them get the price down. Apple Silicon Macs are famous for their world-leading efficiency and their performance. Apple's design philosophy of prioritising the design of the physical product helps to make the Neo feel like a cohesive intentionally designed product, rather than something that was produced simply to fill a gap in the market. 

The laptop also lacks premium features like MagSafe, TouchID being missing on the base model, and the lack of a haptic trackpad. Despite the lack of a haptic trackpad, the Neo doesn't have one of the main problems with non-haptic trackpads, which is their inability to click down anywhere on the surface. Also, due to limitations with the A18 Pro chip, Apple was only able to put one USB-3 port on the Neo, with the second one being USB-2. 

## As always, it's not perfect, and it's not for everyone.

The main issue with the Neo is the specs. It has 8GB of system memory which is on the lower end nowadays. However, the memory speed is fast, so you wouldn't really notice slowdowns until you start running intensive apps or just having a lot of apps open. Apple can't really do much about that, since the memory is bundled within the chip itself. However, the A19 Pro comes with 12GB of memory, which is an improvement, and in the next generation Neo, Apple may use that.

The Neo also suffers from thermal throttling when it's being used intensively for an extended period of time, which means that the system slows down to prevent overheating. Since the Neo is designed for users who do basic computing tasks—such as web browsing and using office software, it's unlikely that most users will experience this.

The Neo isn't designed for intensive usage like programming and video editing, though it handles it decently well if you're just doing things like that occasionally.

macOS is probably my least favourite part of the Neo. Whilst I find it nicer to use than Windows, I personally don't like it all that much. I personally find most desktop systems to be quite clunky and not cohesive. I'll probably write another piece about that.

## What we learn from this

The Neo really proves that a well thought out product can disrupt the market, even with a big budget constraint. Most products, even at higher price points only focus on having good specs on paper without thinking about the physical product that users interact with. Making strategic compromises based on understanding your userbase is far more important, and Apple got it right this time.
